About Me

Who Am I?

Hi, I'm Sai Deogekar. I am a Mechanical Engineer with an interest in Computational Solid Mechanics, Fracture and Failure of Materials and Numerical Methods.

I worked for 2 years at Ansys as a Technical Support Engineer where, in addition to helping customers with technical issues, I worked as a part of the Ansys Innovation Courses team to create educational content in order to train new users and democratize the knowledge of numerical simulations.

I received my PhD in December 2019 from Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, US, where I worked in the Computational Nanomechanics lab with Dr. Catalin Picu. My PhD thesis focused on modeling fibrous materials using various computational tools. I used Finite Element Analysis solvers to study the failure in fibrous materials (such as nonwovens, biological tissues, paper etc.) and investigate the effect of various microstructural parameters on the macroscopic material strength.

In the past, I have studied dynamic brittle fracture in glass, for which I wrote my own code in MATLAB and C. I have been using various computational tools such as MATLAB, Python, ABAQUS etc. for the past nine years.I enjoy learning new topics, programming languages and softwares using online resources to expand my knowledge.

Selected Research

Failure in Soft Materials and Biological Tissues

For my PhD thesis, I worked with Dr. Catalin Picu, to investigate failure in soft fibrous materials and biological tissues due to inter-fiber bond breakage. In the biological world, fibrous structures are found at various length scales, from cytoskeleton of eukaryotic cells at the smaller length scales to multi-functional structures at the tissue scale. In the engineering realm, paper, felt and various non-wovens are just a few examples of fibrous materials.

See more See less

We develop finite element models of fibrous materials and study the effect of various microstructural parameters (such as volume fraction of the fibers, strength of inter-fiber bonds etc.) on the failure mechanisms and macroscopic strength of these materials. Understanding the microscale failure mechanisms of such materials allows engineering stronger synthetic materials and sheds light on damage accumulation in soft tissues, with implications for healing, preventive treatments and the design of artifical tissue constructs.

The figure above shows the finite element model for the fibrous material. The fibers are modeled as Timoshenko beams and crosslinked to each other by bonds. Each inter-fiber bond is composed from a number of fibrils uniformly distributed over the bond area. The bond between two fibers is considered broken when the last fibril breaks. The material parameter uc is proportional to the strength of the bond.

We simulate standard uniaxial tensile test and obtain the following stress-stretch curves.

On the 1-axis we plot the stretch in loading direction and on the 2-axis we have the Cauchy stress normalized by the Young's modulus of the fibers, Ef. The volume fraction of the fibrous material (=volume occupied by fibers/total volume of material) increases from specimen A to D.

We vary the fiber network density ρ (i.e. the total length of fiber per unit material volume), the fiber diameter D and the bond strength uc and observe the following relationships between these microscale parameters and the macroscopic material strength, σc.

We also observe the following relationship between the logarithmic strain at failure εc and the micro-structural parameters.

The main conclusions from this work are summarized below.

  • Macroscopic strength of fibrous material is linearly proportional to its volume fraction and strength of inter-fiber bonds.
  • The strain at failure is inversely related to the volume fraction.
  • As heterogeneity decreases, failure becomes more brittle.
  • Inter-fiber bonds fail predominantly in mode II.

Poisson Contraction and Fiber Kinematics in Biological Tissues

Connective tissue mechanics is highly non-linear and exhibits Poisson ratios much larger than 0.5 (the upper limit of the Poisson ratio for 3D continua). Fiber networks are not continua and hence can exhibit a stronger Poisson effect. In this work, we built finite element models of collagen networks and studied the mechanism leading to the large Poisson effect observed in experiments.

See more See less

The figure below shows the finite element models of three types of networks that we studied: (a) randomly oriented straight fibers (b) randomly oriented tortuous fibers and (c) preferentially oriented straight fibers.

We perform standard uniaxial tensile test in 1-direction. The two curves in the left figure below show the incremental Poisson ratio νi at various values of stretch λ1 for 2 different collagen concentrations. The colored symbols correspond to various experimental data. We use 3D orientation index P2 to quantify the degree of orientation of the fibers. P2 is equal to 0 for a randomly oriented network, while 1 indicates that the fibers are strongly aligned in the loading direction. As the tissue is stretched, more and more fibers reorient in the loading direction (right figure below).

The following relationship is observed between the incremental Poisson ratio νi and the orientation index P2 for models with various levels of pre-alignment.

The main conclusions from this work are summarized below.

  • The large increase in the incremental Poisson ratio as the tissue is stretched, occurs due to strong fiber alignment in the loading direction.
  • Fiber alignment stops when the lateral constraint produced by fibers transverse to the loading direction becomes sufficiently strong.
  • The highest rate of collagen orientation is associated with a transition of network deformation from bending-dominated to axially-dominated.

A Computational Study of Dynamic Brittle Fracture Using the Phase field Method

For my Masters’ thesis, I worked with Dr. Kumar Vemaganti to implement a phase-field approach to study dynamic crack propagation in brittle materials. In this approach the fracture surface is approximated by a phase-field. A continuous scalar-valued phase-field, say c, is introduced in the domain to indicate whether the material is in fractured (c = 0) or unfractured (c = 1) phase. The crack path can be obtained by calculating the evolution of this phase-field, thereby eliminating the need to numerically track the discontinuities. The phase-field approach was implemented initially in MATLAB and was later optimized in C (using the SPOOLES library).

See more See less

The continuous phase-field, c, determines the stiffness of the material (it reduces the strength of the material in the vicinity of the crack). The evolution of this phase-field, in turn, depends on the history of the strain energy of the domain. This couples the momentum and the phase-field evolution equations and the equilibrium state at any given time is given by solving this coupled system of equations.

The biggest advantage of this model is that it does not require algorithmic tracking of the crack tip. This method does not require remeshing of the domain to insert discrete discontinuities (as required in some methods such as eXtended Finite Element Method or XFEM) and no prior knowledge about the crack path is needed either (as is needed in the Cohesive Zone Models). It also captures complex crack behaviour, like branching and interaction of two cracks, very efficiently in both two and three dimensions without introducing any ad hoc criteria for crack nucleation.

Interaction Between Two Cracks in Glass

The geometry of the simulated problem consists of a rectangular plate with two notches on the opposite edges, as shown below. It is subjected to a constant tensile load of σ = 1 MPa on the top and bottom edges.

As seen in the figure below (the blue region denotes the crack paths), the two cracks tend to deviate away from each other initially. However, when branching occurs in both the cracks, the branches which are closer to the other crack get deflected towards the other crack, forming a small island of unfractured material.

The variation of crack length and the crack tip velocity with time for this configuration are shown below.

The horizontal and vertical distance between the crack tips of the initial notches was varied and the simulation was repeated for multiple cases. The simulation results were then compared with observations from literature about interaction between multiple cracks under static or fatigue loading. Experiments and subsequent simulations have shown that in most cases, approaching cracks tend to accelerate due to the interaction except in the presence of a relatively large crack, in which case crack growth might be arrested due to the stress shielding effect. However, our simulation results indicate that for dynamic loading, the presence of another crack does not accelerate the crack propagation. Past experiments have shown that 45% of the Rayleigh wave speed is the upper limit on the velocity of a crack. Post-branching, all four crack tips travel at this maximum speed attainable in the medium.

The main conclusions from this work are summarized below.

  • The phase-field method is shown to be robust and capable of handling a wide range of problems. It is able to model key features of dynamic crack propagation, such as crack branching, without requiring remeshing or prior knowledge of the crack path and is hence, a powerful modeling tool.
  • Under dynamic loading conditions, the presence of another crack does not accelerate crack propagation, unlike the static or fatigue loading case. However, the topology of the crack path is affected by the other crack and depends on the separation between the crack tips.
